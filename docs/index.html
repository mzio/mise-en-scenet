Identifying Mise-en-scène through Machine Learning

A computer-aided approach to discovering the iconic elements of mise-en-scène across film directors and genres.

Main call to action Secondary action

Introduction

In film analysis, a scene's mise-en-scène and cinematography - or anything a director can do to help stage a scene and how he or she shoots a shot - play a significant role in conveying a larger message beyond the explicit plot of a shot. These deliberately chosen elements such as shot depth, colors, lighting, and camera movement can go a long way in conveying a director's specific vision for a scene. While these artistic choices often play a subliminal role in determining how we react and why we feel a certain way in regard to watching a film, as audience members we can also explicitly analyze these shots to get a greater understanding of a director's intended message. In fact, one of the most powerful examples of how these visual elements underlie effective visual storytelling lies in its potential association with the larger aspect of a film's uniqueness and overall genre. Even if we do not know the exact plot of a film sequence, the frenzied, dynamic shots of an action clip can distinguish itself from the more slow and somber stills of a drama. Along another dimension, because mise-en-scène and a film's visual language can play such a large role in getting a director's message across, we can imagine that it might be possible to determine a scene's director just from the visual information contained in that scene alone. Just as authors might have certain writing styles that become apparent across their prose, the countless visual depictions that make up patterns throughout a film might be able to give away directors from their shots alone.

Through such analysis, it could be interesting to discover the iconic elements of directors that show up most frequently or prominently in their work. To get at the latter, another interesting follow-up might be to ask, given the ability to distinguish the directors of frames and scenes by visual information alone, what is the most iconic 30 second shot of a two hour film? By explicitly analyzing these video clips we can then hope to identify a bit of what makes a certain film feel a certain way.


Why use machine learning?

Unfortunately, as a college student definitely growing a deeper appreciation for film but no where close to being a veteran cinephile, identifying the visual elements of single video clip is hard enough. To remember these visual elements across an entire two-hour movie, and then figure out which ones are prominent enough to distinguish a frame's director across a series of movies feels downright impossible. Luckily, this is where machine learning and artificial intelligence can come to the rescue.

Briefly speaking, machine learning involves building computer algorithms that can learn to process data in an intelligent manner on their own. Classic examples of related work involve learning to distinguish pictures of cats and dogs, or identifying the right digit associated with a handwritten number. Although bad at higher level intelligence (e.g. following the plot of a movie from start to finish), these algorithms can excel in cases where they are strictly told to learn one thing and have large amount of data to recognize patterns in. Because they can do all of this in an automated manner, this gives us a basis for comparing a multitude of scenes both within and across different films.

Accordingly, using thirteen of the films screened in class, I first teach a machine learning model to automatically identify a frame's director from the visual information or mise-en-scène alone. Given these films, the model essentially pores over hundreds of shorter video clips sampled from the larger movies, and slowly learns associations between the visual information present in the clip and the fact that the clip was shot by a certain director. We can then identify the most "iconic" scenes by looking at the most recognizable scenes associated with each director according to the model. While as humans we adopt certain language to formalize and define these artistic visual choices, the model learns these associations without knowledge of the definitions or connotations of elements such as lighting, color, or shot type. Based on what the model has discovered from the ground-up using the visual pixel information of the video frames, we then supplement these discoveries with our own interpretation of mise-en-scène.



Learning associations with mise-en-scène

As mentioned earlier, mise-en-scène can refer to anything that a director does to stage a scene and capture a certain type of shot on camera. In this work, we focus however on the elements of mise-en-scène capable of being captured from a single frame. As an example, we can take a look specifically at how lighting and color play a role in setting up a shot.


As an example, in the above shot from Zhang Yimou's To Live, we can immediately note the natural lighting and dusty brown color, courtesy of the environment Fugui finds himself in with regard to the gambling den. This natural lighting and overarching but rather drab color scheme shows up in several additional shots, where even colors that are distinctly supposed to be brighter seem to be muddled by natural shading and an overarching brown. The lack of bright colors conveys a certain grunginess that comes not only with hardship but especially one faced in an ordinary setting, where the difficulties of life have become the norm.



In contrast, Wong Kar-wai's Chungking Express trades this color monotony for pops of color. The lighting is still natural, but the exposure especially in the second half of the film seems much more turned up. We can see the distinct blue and yellow of the second policeman and Faye's shirts, along with various contrasting shades in the background. While the natural lighting gives some indication that their lives are also ordinary, through the color there is a dream-like surrealism that goes along with both the dramatic moody first half and more light-hearted second part of Chungking Express.





Teaching our machine learning model

We just looked at two movies and found visual differences in mise-en-scène that could help us distinguish them from each other. However, now we'd like to see if a machine learning model can learn these visual distinctions as well. Doing so, we can identify the most iconic video clips across many more films according to our model, which might help us characterize the elements of mise-en-scène that define the films and their directors.

To remind ourselves, we want to figure out if we can identify the director of a frame just from the visual information in that frame alone. We call this task classification, as we're essentially trying to determine for each movie frame which class (i.e. the director) a specific frame belongs to. Once we have this, we can try to find the frames that the model has the easiest time classifying, and further look at the mise-en-scène apparent in each of these.

Dataset and film setup

In order to teach our model, we have to essentially curate a large amount of video data. Each data point can be thought of as an individual video frame sampled from its film, as well as a label that in our case denotes the director behind that film. Our model learns by being given a frame, making a prediction for a director by choosing among all possible directors, and then changing its own internal parameters a bit to correct itself if it was wrong. We call this entire process training the model.

For this analysis, we wanted our model to be able to classify the right director for frames from the following films:

  • I Was Born, But... (1932), directed by Yasujirō Ozu
  • The Goddess (1934), directed by Wu Yonggang
  • New Woman (1935), directed by Cai Chusheng
  • Rashomon (1950), directed by Akira Kurosawa
  • The Housemaid (1960), directed by Kim Ki-young
  • Grave of the Fireflies (1988), directed by Isao Takahata
  • The Wedding Banquet (1993), directed by Ang Lee
  • Chungking Express (1994), directed by Wong Kar-wai
  • To Live (1994), directed by Zhang Yimou
  • After Life (1998), directed by Hirokazu Kore-eda
  • Ringu (1998), directed by Hideo Nakata
  • The Hole (1998), directed by Tsai Miing-liang
  • Crouching Tiger Hidden Dragon (2000), directed by Ang Lee

To organize these movies into a video dataset, I first downloaded each movie and split it as evenly as possible into two-minute video clips (the graph above showing the distribution of such clips). Our machine learning model only takes images as input, so for each video clip I additionally sampled a single video frame uniformly across the clip every five seconds. This lead to a total of 9096 video clips to work with.

Visualizing our model results

Because this is a film studies class, the details for actually training our model are out of scope. However, for those interested the code is availble at https://github.com/mzio/mise-en-scenet. However, we can still give some indication that our model was able to classify our frames correctly. To determine whether a certain frame was directed by someone or another, the model essentially learns to represent each frame in a specific manner, where ideally frames shot by the same director end up closer to each other or in clusters.
